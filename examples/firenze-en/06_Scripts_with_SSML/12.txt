<speak version="1.0" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2001/10/synthesis" xmlns:mstts="http://www.w3.org/2001/mstts" xml:lang="en-US"><voice name="en-US-JennyMultilingualNeural">The nave of <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Santa Croce</lang> is essentially a real estate market that ran for five centuries and used marble instead of money. Walk its length and read the floor: tomb after tomb, chapel after chapel, each one purchased by a merchant family that wanted its name in stone for eternity. The <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Bardi</lang>, the <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Peruzzi</lang>, the <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Pazzi</lang> — the banking dynasties of Florence bought their way into consecrated ground with the same logic they applied to everything else. Piety was the product. The transaction was the point.  Find the tomb of Galileo on your left, in the left nave. Then locate the cenotaph of Dante on the opposite side. The distance between them is a few dozen meters. The story connecting them is considerably longer.  Galileo died in 1642, under house arrest at his villa in <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Arcetri</lang>, after the Inquisition had forced him to recant his astronomical findings. The Church prohibited a monumental burial in consecrated ground. His body was placed in a small room near the Novitiate chapel, an informal deposit rather than a grave, without ceremony, without inscription. It stayed there for ninety-five years. The marble tomb you are looking at now, with its busts and its allegorical figures, was erected in 1737, nearly a century after his death, when the political climate had shifted sufficiently for Florence to claim him without embarrassment. <break time="1s"/> The tomb is not a tribute. It is a retrospective rehabilitation, the city deciding, at a safe distance from the controversy, that Galileo was now an asset worth commemorating.  Dante's cenotaph, across the nave, operates on the same logic, with an additional layer of failure. Dante was exiled from Florence in 1302 on political charges, condemned to death in absentia. He died in Ravenna in 1321, and Ravenna buried him and has kept him ever since. The cenotaph in <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Santa Croce</lang> was erected in 1829, five centuries after his death. Florence built a monument to a man it had expelled and never recovered, in a church that had no claim to his remains, with an inscription that makes no mention of the exile or the condemnation. It is a tomb without a body, a tribute without an apology, a city congratulating itself for the genius it once voted to burn. <break time="2s"/>  Machiavelli's cenotaph, a few steps away, completes the pattern. He died in 1527. The monument was erected in 1787. The inscription reads, in Latin, "to so great a name, no eulogy is adequate." His actual burial place, in a family tomb in this church, was lost during <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Vasari</lang>'s renovation of the nave in 1566, when <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Cosimo I</lang> ordered the medieval interior stripped and regularized according to post-Tridentine standards. <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Vasari</lang> demolished the choir screen, removed altars, destroyed fourteenth-century frescoes, and in the process lost track of where Machiavelli was actually buried. The man who wrote the manual for how power operates ended up in an unmarked location beneath the floor of the building his own city later turned into a monument to him.  That renovation also destroyed fragments of <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Orcagna</lang>'s Triumph of Death, and whitewashed <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Giotto</lang>'s frescoes in the <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Peruzzi</lang> chapel, which were only rediscovered and partially restored in the nineteenth century, though the mechanical removal of the lime damaged them significantly. <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Cosimo I</lang>'s aesthetic preferences cost Florence several of its most important medieval paintings.  One last absence worth noting. <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Santa Croce</lang> stands in the quarter that was the center of the <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Ciompi</lang> revolt of 1378, when the wool workers, the laborers at the base of the industry that built every fortune commemorated in this nave, rose against the oligarchy. Inside this building, among the hundreds of inscriptions, monuments, and memorial tablets, there is not a single commemoration of those workers, or of the revolt, or of the class of people whose labor made the marble possible. <break time="2s"/>  Exit through the cloister. The <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Cappella dei <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Pazzi</lang></lang> is at the far end — you can see the unfinished facade from the cloister entrance.</voice></speak>
