No marble, no cladding, no decoration of any kind — just raw sandstone, the same material used for ordinary construction work. This is not how the building was meant to look. In 1516, Pope Leo X, himself a Medici, commissioned Michelangelo to design a marble facade for San Lorenzo. A contract was signed on the nineteenth of January 1518. Michelangelo spent two years quarrying marble in Carrara and Pietrasanta, designing a facade that would have been one of the most elaborate architectural statements of the sixteenth century. On the tenth of March 1520, the contract was cancelled. <break time="1s"/> The extracted marble was partly worked, partly abandoned. The facade remained bare. It has been bare for five hundred years. Like the <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Cappella dei <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Pazzi</lang></lang>, like the campanile's copied reliefs, like Galileo's provisional burial, Florence has a consistent relationship with incompletion: the unfinished surface becomes the monument.  Go inside the basilica briefly if you have time, and find the floor of the presbytery. Set into the stone at the crossing of the transept is a tombstone inscribed: "COSMUS MEDICES HIC SITUS EST DECRETO PUBLICO <phoneme alphabet='ipa' ph='ˈpater ˈpatriae'>PATER PATRIAE</phoneme>." <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Cosimo de' Medici</lang> lies here, by public decree, Father of the Fatherland. He was buried in 1464 beneath the physical center of the church, under the crossing itself, a position reserved for the most sacred burials. The title <phoneme alphabet='ipa' ph='ˈpater ˈpatriae'>Pater Patriae</phoneme>, borrowed directly from Roman civic tradition, was conferred by decree of the Signoria in 1465. A banker from a family of money-changers, buried at the heart of the city's dynastic church with a Roman imperial honorific. The distance traveled from the baptistery competition of 1401, where Giovanni di Bicci was simply one of thirty-four jurors, is measurable in this single inscription.  Now exit toward the <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Cappelle Medicee</lang>, the separate entrance around the left side of the building. You will pass through the Chapel of the Princes first, begun in 1604 and not fully completed until 1962. The walls are encrusted in <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>pietra dure</lang>, the Florentine technique of inlaid semi-precious stone, an overwhelming accumulation of porphyry, granite, and jasper that functions less as decoration than as a declaration of resources. The sarcophagi are largely symbolic; the actual remains are in the crypt below. The cupola was finished in the twentieth century. Even the Medici mausoleum was left incomplete by its commissioners.  The New Sacristy, designed by Michelangelo from 1520, is through the next door. Stand in front of the tombs of Giuliano and Lorenzo de' Medici and notice what the architecture does to your sense of scale. The figures of Dawn, Dusk, Day, and Night recline on curved lids that seem too steep for rest, bodies caught in postures of uncomfortable transition. The architectural membering above them is compressed, the <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>pietra serena</lang> pilasters pushed closer together than classical proportion would allow, the spaces between elements tightened until the room feels as though it is pressing inward. <break time="1s"/> This is deliberate. Michelangelo designed the space to make you feel physically diminished in the presence of the tombs, the architecture itself performing the argument that these men were larger than ordinary human proportion. The room is a machine for manufacturing awe.  Before you leave the complex, note that the Laurentian Library, accessible from the cloister, holds approximately eleven thousand manuscripts commissioned or collected by the Medici, and has been open to the public since 1571. The reading desks still have the chains attached, the metal links used to secure the manuscripts to the furniture. The library that was meant to demonstrate Medici intellectual magnificence kept its books literally in chains. There is a metaphor there, though the Medici would not have recognized it as one. <break time="2s"/>  Come back out through the <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Cappelle Medicee</lang> entrance and cross the Piazza San Lorenzo. The market stalls are in front of you. The <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Mercato Centrale</lang> is two minutes on foot along the <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Via dell'Ariento</lang>.</voice></speak>
