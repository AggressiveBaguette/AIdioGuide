Read it with me: <break time="1s"/> "<lang xml:lang='it-IT'>L'ANTICO CENTRO DELLA CITTÀ DA SECOLARE SQUALLORE A NUOVA VITA RESTITUITO</lang>." <break time="1s"/> The ancient center of the city, restored from centuries of squalor to new life.  Centuries of squalor. <break time="1s"/> That is the official verdict, chiseled in stone in 1895, on everything that stood here before.  What stood here was a neighborhood. A dense, living, breathing quarter called the Mercato Vecchio, and inside it, a Jewish ghetto that had existed since 1571. Between 1885 and 1895, the city of Florence, freshly demoted from its brief role as capital of the new Italian kingdom and desperate to prove its modernity, demolished 341 residential buildings and displaced nearly six thousand people. Around 364 Jewish families were removed from homes their community had occupied for over three centuries. The operation had a name: the <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>risanamento</lang>, the sanitation. The cleanup. The word itself is instructive — it implies the people living there were the contamination.  The new synagogue was built, yes, a few streets away, inaugurated in 1882, before the demolition was even complete. A monument to tolerance, you might say. Or a receipt, confirming the transaction had been arranged before the eviction notices arrived.  And then the arch went up. And the inscription declared victory. No mention of the six thousand. No mention of the ghetto. No mention of anything that had been there before, because the entire political logic of the operation depended on the erasure being total. This is, by any reasonable definition, the earliest documented case of large-scale urban gentrification in modern Europe — carried out not by private developers but by the state itself, in the name of hygiene and aesthetics.  What the arch does not mention, what no plaque on this square mentions, is what happened here on the sixth of November, 1943. German and Italian Fascist forces swept through the neighborhoods surrounding this site. Over three hundred Jewish residents were arrested. Two hundred and forty-three were deported to Auschwitz on the ninth of November. Thirteen came back. <break time="2s"/> The square you are standing in, with its café terraces and its carousel and its tourists photographing the arch, sits on ground where all of this happened. The stone underfoot offers no indication of any of it.  The arch is the tell. Every city has monuments that celebrate what was built. Florence built one that celebrates what was destroyed, and called the destruction an act of restoration. The most cynical part is not the lie — it is how elegant the lie is. Carved in capital letters, in Latin cadence, in the most permanent material available. <break time="2s"/>  Take a moment with that.  <break time="2s"/> When you're ready, turn your back to the arch and walk east along the <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Via dei Calzaiuoli</lang>, the main pedestrian street. In about five minutes, you'll see an octagonal stone building appear at the far end, with the mass of the cathedral rising just behind it. That's where we're going.</voice></speak>
