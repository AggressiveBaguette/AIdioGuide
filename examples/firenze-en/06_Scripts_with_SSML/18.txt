What you are looking at is one of the most important fresco cycles in Western art, and also a document of political persecution.  The paintings on the left and right walls were begun by Masaccio between 1424 and 1428, commissioned by <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Felice Brancacci</lang>, a Florentine merchant and political figure who was not aligned with the Medici. Look at the figures: the bodies have genuine weight, the faces are individualized, the light falls from a single consistent source. This is painting that changed how European artists understood the representation of the human body. Then find the point where the style shifts, where the colors brighten slightly, where the anatomy becomes a little more fluid and decorative. That is where <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Filippino Lippi</lang> took over, completing the cycle between 1481 and 1483. <break time="1s"/> The gap between those two hands is not stylistic evolution. It is fifty years of political exile.  In 1436, <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Cosimo de' Medici</lang> had <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Felice Brancacci</lang> expelled from Florence. The frescoes were left unfinished. The chapel of a man who had been declared an enemy of the dominant family sat incomplete in this church on the wrong side of the Arno for half a century, until the political climate shifted enough for Lippi to be commissioned to finish what Masaccio had started. The seam between their work is visible to the naked eye, a stylistic fracture line that maps directly onto a specific act of political repression.  But the damage did not stop at incompletion. Look carefully at certain faces in the cycle, particularly in areas where the restoration of 1985 to 1989 has documented the original damage. In the years following Brancacci's exile, the Carmelite friars who maintained the chapel effaced portraits of the Brancacci family from the frescoes, scraping away faces and figures associated with the disgraced patron. A scene of the Crucifixion of Peter was destroyed entirely. Under raking light, the restorations are visible as patches of flat, uniform pigment where the original painted surface was abraded down to the plaster. The Brancacci Chapel is here rather than in <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Santa Croce</lang> or San Lorenzo precisely because its patron was not part of the inner circle. The greatest painting of the early fifteenth century ended up in a working-class parish on the far bank of the river, unfinished for fifty years, its patron's face scraped off the wall. Florence's relationship with its own masterpieces has always been complicated by who paid for them and who fell from favor afterward.
