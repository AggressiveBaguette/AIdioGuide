Florence decided its masterpieces were too valuable to leave outside in the rain — which is, when you think about it, a perfectly rational decision, and also a quietly deflating one.  But the story of how these doors came to exist is worth your attention, because it has almost nothing to do with artistic genius and everything to do with institutional power.  The year is 1401. The <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Arte di Calimala</lang>, the guild of cloth merchants and international textile traders, the wealthiest professional corporation in Florence, decides it wants a new set of bronze doors for the baptistery's north entrance. So it does what any serious institution does when it needs something built: it runs a competition. Seven sculptors are invited to submit a trial panel, each depicting the same subject, the sacrifice of Isaac. The judges are thirty-four men, drawn from the guild. One of them, just one among thirty-four, is a banker named <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Giovanni di Bicci de' Medici</lang>. <break time="1s"/> At this point, the Medici are not patrons of the arts. They are jurors on a committee.  The two panels that survive from that competition are now at the <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Bargello</lang> museum, a short walk from here. <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Brunelleschi</lang>'s and <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Ghiberti</lang>'s, side by side. The guild chose <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Ghiberti</lang>. <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Brunelleschi</lang>, the story goes, left for Rome. The doors that followed, and then the eastern doors that Michelangelo would later call the Gates of Paradise, though that name came from <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Vasari</lang>, not Michelangelo himself, took <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Ghiberti</lang> the better part of his working life.  Now, if you have a museum pass, go around the baptistery to the left and step inside. On the north-west interior wall, you will find a tomb that tells you everything about how the Medici actually operated. It is the funerary monument of <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Baldassarre Cossa</lang>, who had been Pope John the Twenty-Third, until the Council of Constance deposed him in 1415 and declared his papacy illegitimate. Cossa was a close ally of the Medici bank. When he died in 1419, <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Cosimo de' Medici</lang> commissioned <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Donatello</lang> and <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Michelozzo</lang> to build him a monument inside the baptistery itself, one of the most sacred civic buildings in Florence. The work was completed between 1422 and 1428.  Look at the inscription on the sarcophagus. It reads: "<phoneme alphabet='ipa' ph='joˈannes ˈkwondam ˈpaːpa viˈdʒɛsimus ˈtɛrtsius'>Ioannes quondam papa XXIII</phoneme>." <break time="1s"/> Quondam. Formerly. Once-pope. That single Latin word was the result of a diplomatic negotiation with the reigning Pope Martin the Fifth, who objected to any language that might imply residual legitimacy. So they split the difference. Cosimo got his monument for his deposed ally. Rome got its qualifier. The stone got carved with a compromise.
