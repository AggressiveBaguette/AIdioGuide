<speak version="1.0" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2001/10/synthesis" xmlns:mstts="http://www.w3.org/2001/mstts" xml:lang="en-US"><voice name="en-US-JennyMultilingualNeural">That building right there — the one that looks like a cross between a granary and a church, because it was both — is <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Orsanmichele</lang>, and the story written on its exterior walls is the story of how Florence actually worked.  Look at the niches cut into the facade at street level, running all the way around the building. Each one was assigned to a specific guild by city decree in 1339, and each guild was required to fill its niche with a bronze statue of its patron saint. Required. On a deadline. At its own expense. <break time="1s"/> This was not piety. This was a compulsory public demonstration of financial capacity, organized by the city as a kind of competitive display. The guild that commissioned the most impressive statue, in the most expensive material, by the most celebrated sculptor, won a round of a game that never really ended.  The <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Arte di Calimala</lang>, the cloth merchants you met at the baptistery, hired <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Ghiberti</lang> for their Saint John the Baptist. The linen workers' guild, the <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Linaioli</lang>, commissioned <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Donatello</lang> for their Saint Mark. The names of the sculptors tell you the budget. Bronze tells you the ambition.  But to understand why this competition was so intense, you need to understand what these guilds actually were. Florence's economy in the fourteenth century ran on three interlocking systems: the <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Arte della Lana</lang> processed raw wool into finished cloth, the <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Arte di Calimala</lang> imported and refined foreign fabrics, and the <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Arte del Cambio</lang> ran the international currency exchange and credit networks that financed half of Europe's trade. These were not trade associations. They were the effective government of the city's economy, with their own courts, their own regulations, their own foreign offices. The Florentine republic was, in many ways, a corporation that had learned to issue laws.  What the niches of <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Orsanmichele</lang> make visible is the hierarchy of that system, frozen in bronze and stone, at eye level, on a public street. Wealth announcing itself as devotion, and devotion serving as a ledger of competitive standing. <break time="2s"/>  Keep walking south on the <lang xml:lang='it-IT'>Via dei Calzaiuoli</lang>.
