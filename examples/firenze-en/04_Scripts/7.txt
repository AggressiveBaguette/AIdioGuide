Find somewhere to sit, or at least somewhere to stand where you will not be swept along by the crowd, because this room requires your full attention.

You are in the Salone dei Cinquecento, the Hall of the Five Hundred. Look up. <break time="1s"/> The ceiling is covered in thirty-nine painted panels, Vasari's work, commissioned between 1563 and 1565 by Cosimo I de' Medici. The central inscription reads "COSIMUS MEDICES DVX FLORENTINORUM." The scenes depict military triumphs: the conquest of Pisa, the conquest of Siena. Stylized battles, heroic commanders, no civilian dead. A ceiling-sized victory parade, paid for by the man who ordered the campaigns.

But the ceiling is only half the story. The other half is what lies underneath it, literally and politically.

This room was built in 1494 and 1495, on the orders of Savonarola, as the chamber of the Grand Council of the Florentine Republic. Five hundred citizens, assembled here to govern. The room was physically created by the expulsion of the Medici, which happened in November 1494. Its very existence was an act of republican assertion. In 1503, the Gonfalonier Soderini commissioned Leonardo da Vinci and Michelangelo simultaneously to paint competing frescos on these walls, depicting Florentine military victories, propaganda for the republic, funded by the republic. Leonardo abandoned his after a technical failure with his paint medium. Michelangelo was recalled to Rome by Pope Julius II before he finished. Neither fresco survives in any visible form.

When Cosimo I reclaimed the palace in the 1540s, he did not simply redecorate. He raised the ceiling by approximately seven meters and had Vasari paint over everything. The republican room became a ducal throne room. The gesture was so complete that most visitors never know the room beneath the room existed. <break time="2s"/>

There is one detail worth knowing, if you are the kind of person who finds unresolved mysteries satisfying. In one of Vasari's battle scenes, a small banner bears the words "cerca trova," seek and you will find. A researcher named Maurizio Seracini spent decades arguing that this was a clue, that Leonardo's lost fresco had been preserved behind a thin wall built in front of the original surface before Vasari painted over it. Between 2011 and 2012, with funding from National Geographic, micro-probes were inserted into the wall cavity. They found traces of a black pigment consistent with Leonardo's known paint chemistry. Then the Florentine cultural authorities suspended the research. It has not resumed. Whether this was caution, politics, or institutional inertia depends on who you ask. The wall is still there. The fresco, if it exists, is still behind it.

Now walk toward the far end of the room and find the door to the Studiolo of Francesco I. It is small, almost hidden. That is the point.

The Studiolo was built between 1570 and 1572, a room of roughly three by five meters, with no windows and a concealed entrance set into a painted panel. Thirty-four paintings on wood, eight bronze statues, an iconographic program designed by the court intellectual Vincenzo Borghini around the four elements, alchemy, and the idea of the prince as mediator between nature and science. It was Francesco's private archive for precious objects, and also a piece of political compensation: the paintings present him as a philosopher-prince, a sovereign of intellect and refinement. The reality was somewhat different. Francesco was largely uninterested in governing and delegated most actual power to his brother Ferdinando.

Francesco died on the nineteenth of October 1587, at the villa of Poggio a Caiano. His wife Bianca Cappello died eleven hours later. The official diagnosis was malaria. The historical suspicion, which was always directed at Ferdinando, who inherited the Grand Duchy and promptly abandoned his cardinal's robes to take power, was arsenic poisoning. In 2006, a team from the University of Florence analyzed Francesco's hair and bones and Bianca's preserved viscera. They found arsenic levels consistent with acute poisoning. Ferdinando had motive, means, and timing. He also, after their deaths, imposed a damnatio memoriae on Bianca, denied her public funeral rites, and removed her portraits from the palaces. The man who may have killed her also erased her. <break time="2s"/>

Before you leave, consider the building itself one more time. This palace has been, in sequence: the seat of the Florentine Republic, a prison, a ducal court, a parliamentary chamber when Florence briefly served as capital of unified Italy in the 1860s, and now a museum that also functions as the active city hall of Florence. Cosimo the Elder was held in a cell in the tower here in 1433, before his exile and his triumphant return. The same cells were used for political opponents for generations afterward.

And in 1478, after the Pazzi Conspiracy failed to kill Lorenzo de' Medici, Archbishop Salviati was dragged to the windows of the piano nobile, the floor just above street level on the facade facing the piazza, and hanged in his vestments in full public view. The windows are still there. The republic that ordered the hanging called itself a government of laws.

Every institution that has ever occupied this building has used it the same way: as a machine for making power look inevitable. The frescos change. The logic does not. <break time="2s"/>

Come back down and exit through the main entrance. The Loggia dei Lanzi is immediately to your right. Find the Perseus, and look up at him from below.
