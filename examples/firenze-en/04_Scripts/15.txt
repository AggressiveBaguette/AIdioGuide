Run your hand along the lower facade if you are standing close enough. The stone at street level is rough, heavily rusticated, each block projecting outward in irregular bosses. As your eye travels upward, the surface becomes progressively smoother, more refined, more restrained. That graduation is not decorative. It is a calculated social signal, and it was chosen with considerable deliberation.

When Cosimo de' Medici decided to build a new family palace in 1444, Brunelleschi submitted a design. Cosimo rejected it as too regal, too overtly princely for a man who held no official title and governed a republic that had, within living memory, exiled his own father. He gave the commission instead to Michelozzo, with instructions that produced this: a building that reads as solid, serious, and prosperous, but stops well short of the architectural language of a palace. No columns, no triumphal arches, no explicit dynastic symbolism on the exterior. In a city that had exiled the Albizzi family for excessive ambition and would exile the Medici themselves twice, the choice of rough stone over polished marble was a political calculation disguised as an aesthetic preference. <break time="1s"/> Humility, in Florence, was a survival strategy.

Now go inside and find the Cappella dei Magi, the small chapel on the upper floor. The walls are covered in a fresco cycle painted by Benozzo Gozzoli in 1459, depicting the procession of the Three Kings. Look at the faces. The figures in this biblical cavalcade are portraits: members of the Medici family, their allies, their clients, their dependents, all dressed as Magi, all riding through a idealized Tuscan landscape toward the Christ child. Lorenzo de' Medici appears as one of the kings. The family that had no official title, that had built its palace in deliberately modest stone to avoid the appearance of royal ambition, had commissioned a private chapel in which they were painted as the men who brought gifts to God. <break time="1s"/> The humility was strictly for the exterior.

The chapel was private, accessible only to the family and their most intimate guests. The fresco was not public propaganda in the way the Perseus or the Salone dei Cinquecento were public propaganda. It was something more intimate and arguably more revealing: a private self-image, the Medici as they wished to see themselves when no republic was watching. Kings by divine association, legitimized not by law or election, but by proximity to the sacred, rendered in gold leaf and lapis lazuli on a wall that almost no one outside the family would ever see. <break time="2s"/>

Turn left as you exit the palazzo, then left again onto the Piazza San Lorenzo. The basilica is fifty meters ahead.
