From up here, the city arranges itself into the postcard it has been perfecting for five centuries.

Take a moment and locate the landmarks one by one. The dome, Brunelleschi's solution to the impossible problem, rising above everything else on the north bank. Just to its right, the campanile, where the guild of cloth merchants commissioned the cycle of human knowledge in stone. The tower of the Palazzo Vecchio, the republican fortress that became a ducal instrument, its crenellations still medieval against the skyline. The silver ribbon of the Arno cutting the city in two. And there, the Ponte Vecchio, the one bridge the Wehrmacht left standing in August 1944, where the butchers were expelled so the duke's corridor would not smell of blood.

You can trace almost every stop of this tour from where you are standing. The city lays itself out with remarkable legibility from this height.

The hill you are standing on has its own history. During the siege of Florence in 1529 and 1530, when Charles V's imperial forces surrounded the city, Michelangelo directed the construction of defensive bastions on this ridge. The same man who left San Lorenzo's facade bare and designed the New Sacristy to make visitors feel small spent months engineering earthworks up here to keep an army out. The bastions are partially visible below the Viale Galileo if you know where to look. The piazzale itself was created in 1869, during the period when Florence served as provisional capital of unified Italy, and named for Michelangelo as an instrument of national legitimization by the new Savoy state, which needed Florentine cultural prestige to anchor its own fragile identity. The inscriptions on the square speak the language of the Risorgimento. They do not mention the Florentine commune, or the republic, or the Medici.

But the view is what deserves your full attention now, because the view is the argument, and the argument is a very old one.

What you can see from here is the Florence that survived, was restored, was preserved, was curated: the marble, the towers, the bridges, the domes. What the panorama does not show you is the six thousand people expelled from the Piazza della Repubblica so the triumphal arch could go up. It does not show the Brancacci family's faces scraped off their own chapel walls. It does not show the Pazzi coat of arms chiseled off every surface in the city by decree. It does not show Archbishop Salviati hanging from the Palazzo Vecchio windows in his vestments, or Galileo's nine years of house arrest on the hill behind you, or the two hundred and forty-three people deported from the streets below on the ninth of November 1943, of whom thirteen came back.

The view from the Piazzale Michelangelo is the most persuasive thing Florence has ever produced, and Florence has produced extraordinary things. It is a city that learned, earlier than almost any other, that beauty is the most durable form of argument, and that a sufficiently magnificent skyline can make an audience forget to ask what was demolished to clear the sightline. <break time="2s"/>

You have been looking at the scars all day. You know where they are now. The view is still beautiful. Both things are true, and Florence would be the first to tell you that holding two contradictory truths simultaneously is the beginning of intelligence. It may also be the city's oldest survival strategy.
