If you are standing on the upper floor, look around you: five hundred seats, premium food counters, a cooking school, exposed ironwork from the original 1874 structure repurposed as atmosphere. Then lean over the railing and look down at the ground floor, where the traditional vendors still operate — vegetables, tripe, cheese, cuts of meat, the actual food economy of a working city.

That vertical distance of about ten meters is the story of Florence in the twenty-first century, compressed into a single building.

The upper floor was renovated in 2014 and converted into a food hall aimed squarely at the tourist and upper-middle-class market. The same year, eighty-three street vendors were expelled from the immediate perimeter of the San Lorenzo basilica by the municipal government, in the name of urban decor, echoing a justification Florence has been using since 1865, when the Piazza della Repubblica project began clearing the old market and the ghetto in the name of hygiene and aesthetics. The formula is consistent across six centuries: the existing commercial activity is reclassified as disorder, the people conducting it are relocated or expelled, and the space is reopened at a higher price point.

The medieval wool trade that built the fortunes commemorated in every church and palace on this tour operated on the same territorial logic: control the supply chain, control the workspace, extract the margin. The Arte della Lana did not call it gentrification. They called it guild regulation. The effect on the workers at the bottom of the chain was comparable. <break time="1s"/> The Ciompi who revolted in 1378 had no stall in this building and no seat on the upper floor. Their descendants, in a manner of speaking, are the traditional vendors on the ground level, watching the food court fill up above them.

Florence has always known how to monetize its own identity. The only thing that changes is what gets sold. <break time="2s"/>

Come back down and leave the market. Take the Via Nazionale west, then cross the Arno at the Ponte alla Carraia or the Ponte Amerigo Vespucci. Follow the Borgo Santo Spirito up toward the piazza — about twenty minutes on foot.
