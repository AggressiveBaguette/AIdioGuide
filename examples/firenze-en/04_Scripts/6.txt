Somewhere near the fountain — the oversized Neptune that Florentines nicknamed "Il Biancone," the big white one, with the popular verdict on its sculptor summarized in a rhyme: "Ammannato, Ammannato, quanto marmo hai sciupato" — wasted marble, they said — somewhere near that fountain, set into the paving stones, there is a small circular plaque. <break time="1s"/> Look for it. It is often stepped on, sometimes covered by market stalls or tourist equipment. That near-invisibility is almost fitting.

The plaque marks the exact spot where Girolamo Savonarola was hanged and burned on the twenty-third of May, 1498. But the date alone does not tell you the full story. To get the full story, you need to go back exactly one year and three months.

On the seventh of February 1497, Savonarola's followers, known as the Piagnoni, the Wailers, moved through the streets of Florence conducting house-to-house searches. They confiscated books, mirrors, cosmetics, paintings, musical instruments, anything they had classified as sinful or vain. They brought it all here, to this piazza, and they built a bonfire. The Bonfire of the Vanities. It was an act of mass cultural destruction organized with the administrative efficiency of a guild operation, and it happened in the city that had spent the previous century commissioning the most sophisticated secular art in Europe.

Florence burned its own library. <break time="1s"/> And then, fourteen months later, on this same paving, at this same point in the city's central square, Florence burned the man who had organized the burning. The charges were heresy and sedition. The method was identical: a scaffold, a rope, and fire. The city applied to Savonarola the exact logic he had applied to the books.

There is something almost too neat about that symmetry, which is probably why the plaque is so small. Large monuments invite contemplation. A small disc in the floor, frequently obscured, invites nothing. You either know what you are stepping over, or you do not. <break time="2s"/>

Now look up. The facade of the Palazzo Vecchio is directly in front of you. Find the windows of the piano nobile, the upper floor above the main entrance. In 1478, the body of Archbishop Salviati was thrown from one of those windows, a rope around his neck, still in his vestments. That story, and the conspiracy that produced it, begins the moment you step inside. Follow me in.
