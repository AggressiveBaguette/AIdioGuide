Run your hand along the facade if you are close enough to touch it. Those dark green bands of marble have a specific origin. They come from quarries near Prato, a city about seventeen kilometers northwest of here, which Florence purchased in 1351 for seventeen thousand five hundred florins. <break time="1s"/> Purchased. A city. Like a piece of real estate. And then, with the quiet confidence of a landlord redecorating, Florence stripped its new acquisition's stone and built it into the walls of its cathedral for the next five centuries. Every time you look at this facade, you are looking at a territorial claim made permanent in marble.

The green is serpentine from the hills of Figline di Prato. The white is Carrara marble. The pink is from the Maremma. This building is a geological map of Florentine dominance, and it was designed to be read that way, from a distance, from the road, from the hills, by anyone approaching the city and needing to understand, before they even arrived, who was in charge.

The logic was simple and ruthless: a cathedral visible for miles is not a religious building, it is a political statement. Construction began in 1296. To make room for it and for this piazza, entire city blocks were demolished, workshops cleared, a cemetery relocated. The open space you are standing in right now is anomalous for a medieval city of this density. That emptiness is the footprint of everything that was torn down to make the statement legible.

And then there is the facade itself, which carries its own peculiar history. The original medieval facade was demolished in 1587 on the orders of Francesco I de' Medici, who found it aesthetically inadequate. What replaced it was nothing. The cathedral stood with a bare brick front for nearly three hundred years, a gaping absence at the heart of the city's most important building. The marble facade you are looking at now was inaugurated on the twelfth of May, 1887. It is a nineteenth-century invention dressed in medieval clothes, and if you look carefully at the junction between the facade and the older flanks of the building, the stylistic discontinuity is not subtle. <break time="1s"/> They are simply not the same building pretending to be one.

Underneath all of this, if you go inside and descend the stairs between the first pillars, you will find the remains of Santa Reparata, a paleochristian basilica that the cathedral was built directly on top of. Excavated between 1965 and 1973, open to the public since 1974. Down there, under the floor of the floor, you can see Roman mosaics and the tomb of Brunelleschi, the man who solved the problem of the dome after everyone else had given up. He is buried beneath the building his dome made famous, in a crypt that most visitors walk over without knowing it exists.

Florence has always been better at display than at acknowledgment. <break time="2s"/>
