In a glass case on the upper floor of this museum, mounted on a small ornate base, is a human finger. The middle finger of Galileo Galilei's right hand, removed from his body in 1737 during the transfer of his remains to Santa Croce, ninety-five years after his death. <break time="1s"/> It is displayed upright, pointing toward the ceiling, which may or may not be intentional, but which Florence has chosen not to correct.

The gesture of preserving a scientist's finger as though it were the relic of a saint is the most Florentine thing in this city, and this city has produced considerable competition for that title.

Here is the sequence of events. Galileo spent the last years of his life under house arrest at his villa in Arcetri, on the hills south of Florence, condemned by the Roman Inquisition in 1633 after his trial in Rome. He had published his defense of the Copernican model of the solar system. The Church found him vehemently suspected of heresy and forced him to recant. The Medici, specifically Grand Duke Ferdinando II, who had been Galileo's patron and protector for years, did not intervene effectively. When Rome applied pressure, Florence yielded. <break time="1s"/> Galileo lived out the remaining nine years of his life at the Villa il Gioiello in Arcetri, going blind, still working, still corresponding with the scientists of Europe, but confined to a hillside outside the city whose rulers had financed his telescopes and then declined to defend his conclusions.

He died in 1642. Pope Urban VIII made clear that a monumental public burial in consecrated ground was not acceptable for a convicted heretic. Ferdinando II again deferred to Rome. The body was placed in a small side room near the Novitiate chapel of Santa Croce, without ceremony, without inscription, a provisional deposit that was intended to be temporary and remained in place for nearly a century.

In 1737, when the political and ecclesiastical climate had shifted sufficiently, Florence finally erected the marble tomb you saw in Santa Croce. During the transfer of the remains, three fingers, a tooth, and a vertebra were removed and kept as relics. Two fingers and the tooth are in other collections. The middle finger ended up here.

The Medici had funded Galileo's research, housed his instruments, given him a title and a salary, and celebrated his discoveries as long as celebrating them cost nothing. When the Inquisition made the cost explicit, they paid it with Galileo's freedom rather than with their own political capital. The finger in the glass case is the monument to that calculation, preserved in crystal with the same reverence Florence applied to the bones of martyrs, pointing at nothing in particular, or perhaps at everything. <break time="2s"/>
