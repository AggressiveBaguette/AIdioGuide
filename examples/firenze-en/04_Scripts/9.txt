At the southern end of the bridge, look up at the corner tower, the one the corridor visibly wraps around on protruding stone brackets, jutting out over the river rather than passing through the structure. That is the Torre Mannelli, and it is there because the Mannelli family refused. <break time="1s"/> When Cosimo I ordered Vasari to build a private elevated corridor connecting the Uffizi to the Palazzo Pitti in 1565, the project required passing through or over whatever stood in its way. Most property owners complied, or were made to comply. The Mannelli did not. They refused expropriation, and Cosimo, for reasons that history does not fully explain, accepted the refusal. Vasari built around them, cantilevering the corridor on stone brackets over the corner of the tower. The detour is still visible today, an architectural scar that marks the one time a Florentine family successfully said no to a Medici duke.

The corridor itself was built in five months, according to Vasari's own account, which is either a remarkable feat of construction or a remarkable feat of self-promotion, possibly both. Its function was straightforward: the Medici could move between their administrative palace, their private residence, and the Uffizi without ever descending to street level, without exposure to crowds, without vulnerability to the kind of attack that had nearly killed Lorenzo de' Medici in 1478. A private road, above the city, invisible to those below.

The shops on the bridge at the time were occupied by butchers and tanners. The smell of blood and curing hides drifted upward into the corridor. In 1593, Ferdinando I issued a decree expelling every butcher and tanner from the Ponte Vecchio. The official justification was that the odors were offensive to the ducal passage above. They were replaced by goldsmiths and jewelers, whose work produced no smell, and whose rents were considerably higher. <break time="1s"/> The bridge you are standing on, lined exclusively with gold and jewelry shops, is the direct result of a hygiene complaint from a corridor that no ordinary citizen was permitted to enter. The gentrification of the Ponte Vecchio was decreed from above, literally.

One final note about this bridge, which belongs to a different century entirely. In August 1944, the Wehrmacht ordered the destruction of every bridge over the Arno in Florence as part of their retreat. Every bridge was blown up. Every bridge except this one, which survived intact, reportedly on a direct order from Hitler himself, possibly mediated by the German consul Gerhard Wolf. The medieval structure that Ferdinando I had sanitized for ducal comfort was spared by the army that destroyed everything around it. Florence has spent considerable energy interpreting that fact ever since, without arriving at a conclusion that satisfies everyone. <break time="2s"/>

Cross the bridge and continue straight on the Via Guicciardini. In five minutes, the massive facade of the Palazzo Pitti fills the end of the street.
